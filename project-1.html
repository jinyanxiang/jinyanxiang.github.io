TL;DR
Peer providers in the sharing economy are significantly less willing to serve consumers from high-inequality regions — not because of who those consumers are, but because of where they're from. The mechanism: high inequality → inferred low SES → lower perceived trustworthiness → discrimination. Platforms can counteract this with smarter matching algorithms and similarity score displays.
Background
The Problem

The sharing economy's rapid expansion has not translated into equal access for all consumers. Many face systematic screening by peer providers based on race, gender, sexual orientation, and other demographic characteristics. This research asks: does a consumer's regional economic inequality add another layer of vulnerability?

Location information is openly displayed on platform profiles — city and state on Airbnb and Turo, state alone on Lending Club and Prosper. This creates a new, overlooked channel for discrimination.

Research Question & Hypotheses
How Does Regional Inequality Shape Access?

How does economic inequality in a consumer's region — from local community to national level — influence their access to peer-to-peer services?

H1
Providers are less willing to serve consumers from high- vs. low-inequality regions.
H2
This effect is driven by lower perceived SES → lower trust for consumers from high-inequality regions.

Methodology

Conclusion
What This Means

Despite explicit anti-discrimination policies, these findings confirm that regional economic inequality is a new, overlooked axis of discrimination in the sharing economy — underscoring the urgent need for platforms and regulators to act beyond current demographic-focused frameworks.

Managerial Implications
What Platforms Can Do
Enhanced Matching Algorithm
Incorporate self-disclosed provider and consumer information into similarity scores — complementing existing preference and booking history data. Use with caution around demographic features to avoid introducing new biases.
Similarity Score Display
Surface a visible provider–consumer similarity score to draw attention away from bias-susceptible signals like location — and help discrimination-prone consumers overcome the cold-start problem by securing initial reviews.
Regulatory Extension
Existing anti-discrimination frameworks focus on demographics. Regulators should extend protections to region-based screening — a currently unaddressed grey area that disproportionately harms consumers from economically unequal regions.
