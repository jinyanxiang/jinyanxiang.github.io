TL;DR
Rising economic inequality pushes people toward college and higher-earning majors — not because of genuine interest, but because inequality inflates the perceived return on education. This has direct implications for the student debt crisis and the collapse of the humanities.
Background
Education Decisions in an Unequal World

"Behind these skyrocketing numbers, a multitude of young people and their families are making 'complex, vexing and consequential' education decisions."

— Grewal, Meyer, & Mittal (2023)

Beyond micro- and meso-level factors — such as individual habitus and school and community environment — this research asks: how does a fundamental macroeconomic factor, namely societal economic inequality, shape individuals' educational decisions through the lens of perceived education premium?

Propositions
Two Pathways of Influence
College Decision
Economic inequality encourages individuals to pursue higher education by amplifying the perceived education premium — as inequality grows, the income gap between college and high school graduates widens in people's minds.
Choice of Major
Economic inequality drives individuals toward majors with higher earning potential, even at the expense of personal interest — reflecting a shift from intrinsic to extrinsic motivation driven by a widening perceived income gap across fields.

Methodology
Four Studies
01
Google Trends
State-Level Search Interest
Data
State-level Google Trends for college-related terms (2015–2019, 50 US states + DC)
Analysis
Correlation & regression
For every 0.05-point increase in a state's Gini index, collective search interest in college education rises by approximately 17.82 points (scale: 0–100).
02
Census Panel Data
County-Level Enrollment
Data
ACS enrollment data, ages 18–24, 2010–2019 (831 counties / 3,141 counties)
Analysis
Fixed-effects GLM with logit link & binomial family; sensitivity analyses
As county-level Gini rises, a greater proportion of 18–24 year-olds enroll in college or graduate school. Robust to unobserved confounding.
03
Decision Game Experiment
College Attendance Decision
Sample
Amazon Mechanical Turk respondents
Design
One-way between-subject experiment with manipulated economic inequality
Analysis
Chi-square, mediation analysis
A significantly greater proportion (+20.5 percentage points) of individuals chose to attend college when societal inequality is high vs. low. Perceived education premium confirmed as the mechanism.
04
Tradeoff Experiment
Major Choice Tradeoff
Sample
Cloud Research respondents
Design
2×3 mixed design — manipulated inequality × counterbalanced major tradeoff pairs
Analysis
T-test, mixed ANOVA, mediation analysis
Individuals are significantly more likely to choose higher-earning majors over personally meaningful ones when inequality is high. Perceived education premium confirmed as the mechanism.
Conclusion
What This Means

Rising economic inequality motivates individuals to pursue higher education and to prioritize earning potential over personal interests when choosing a major. These findings carry important policy implications for several pressing challenges in higher education.

Policy Implications
Two Pressing Higher Education Crises
The Student Debt Crisis
Inequality can pressure young people into college without adequately weighing their finances — while simultaneously inflating the perceived education premium, fostering undue optimism about repaying debt.

NUDGE: Help students consider career paths and financial capabilities beyond the inflated premium.
The Humanities Crisis
Humanities degrees fell to less than one-third of their 1970s share, and nearly 50% of humanities graduates regret their major (vs. 25% of engineering graduates).

REFRAME: Emphasize the earning power of transferable skills — critical thinking, communication, adaptability — and foster environments where students build on authentic strengths.
