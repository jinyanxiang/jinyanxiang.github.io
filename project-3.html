TL;DR
People instinctively associate economic inequality with poverty rather than affluence — yet in reality, rising inequality is driven more by growth at the top. This perception-reality gap helps explain why many who see inequality as unjust still oppose redistribution from the rich.

While prior research extensively examines how people perceive the level of inequality, little is known about what inequality means to them.

Do they associate rising economic inequality more with an increase in poverty — or in affluence?

Implicit Association Test (IAT)
Implicit Associations
Sample
Amazon Mechanical Turk respondents
Measure
D-score — standardized difference in reaction time when inequality is paired with poverty vs. affluence
Analysis
One-sample t-test against zero to assess implicit association; robustness confirmed across demographic subgroups (gender, race, SES, political ideology)
How the IAT Works
Measuring Implicit Associations Between Inequality & Income Groups

Congruent

↑ Faster responses
↕  reaction time difference = strength of implicit association
Block B
Incongruent
Inequality Affluence paired together
↓ Slower responses

Participants categorize words faster when concepts feel naturally linked. Faster sorting in Block A signals a stronger implicit association between inequality and poverty.

Conclusion
What This Means

People associate income inequality more with poverty than affluence — and this lay understanding extends beyond income inequality to the abstract notion of inequality itself. These findings provide important implications for policy making.

Policy Implications
Two Insights for Policymakers
A New Form of Misperception
People's lay understanding of inequality — associating it more with poverty than affluence — is opposite to reality: rising inequality is driven predominantly by growth at the top of the income distribution. This perception-reality gap represents a previously overlooked form of misperception with direct policy consequences.
Explaining a Policy Paradox
A majority of Americans (55%) view the wealth distribution as unjust — yet over two-thirds oppose redistribution from the rich to the poor. Our findings help explain this paradox: because people mentally link inequality with poverty rather than affluence, taxing the rich feels like the wrong fix — and direct support for those at the bottom feels more intuitive.
