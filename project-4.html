Choosing to Make a Difference:
How Choice Mindset Shapes Environmental Support

Reveals how choice mindset — the salience of choice in people's minds — affects support for the environment, and what this means for how policymakers, NGOs, and companies can nudge people toward environmental action.

TL;DR
The more salient choice feels to people, the more they support environmental causes — donating more, endorsing environmental taxes, and clicking environmental ads more. Adding just two words ("choose to") to an ad boosted clicks by 12%. A low-cost, scalable nudge for governments, NGOs, and brands.
Background
A Theoretical Contradiction

Prior theories make conflicting predictions about whether making choice salient — choice mindset, the extent to which people construe actions as choices — would increase or decrease environmental support. This research reconciles the contradiction.

Competing Theoretical Predictions
↑ Predicts Increase
Choice mindset activates a sense of personal agency and autonomy — making people feel empowered to act and more motivated to support causes.
VS
↓ Predicts Decrease
Choice mindset triggers individualistic thinking — reducing concern for collective problems like the environment and lowering prosocial motivation.
This research shows: choice mindset consistently increases environmental support — resolving the contradiction across survey, donation, and field experiment settings.
Methodology & Results
Four Studies
01
Survey
Support for Environmental Tax
Sample
Nationally representative sample via Qualtrics — proportional quota sampling stratified by gender, ethnicity, religion, and political ideology
Analysis
Correlation & regression
There is a significant positive correlation between perceived choice salience and support for environmental taxes — including disposable cup charges, plastic bag fees, and gas tax increases.
02
Survey
Donations to Environmental Causes
Sample
Amazon Mechanical Turk respondents
Analysis
Tobit regression — to address the zero-inflated, non-negative, continuous nature of donation amount
The more salient the choice feels, the more people donate to environmental advocacy groups, even after controlling for people's social status and political orientation.
03
Field Experiment · Facebook A/B Test
Consumption of Environmental Content in Social Media
Design
6-day Facebook A/B test, $44/day budget, UK users aged 18–64, newsfeed placement only
Reach
36,978 users · 1,925 link clicks · 131 likes · 80 shares
Analysis
Two-sample proportion z test comparing click-through rates
Adding just two words — "choose to" — to an environmental ad resulted in a 12% boost in click-through rate. Pre-tested to show no difference in perceived attractiveness.
Version A — Control
🌱  "Make a difference. Support environmental causes today."
Baseline click-through rate
Version B — Treatment
🌱  "Choose to make a difference. Support environmental causes today."
↑ +12% click-through rate
04
Google Trends · Archival Data
State-Level Choice Interest & Eco-Friendliness
Data
State-level Google Trends for choice-related searches (2015–2020, 50 US states + DC) + Archival eco-friendliness scores
Analysis
Stepwise regression
States with higher search interest in choice-related terms tend to have significantly higher eco-friendly scores — even after controlling for covariates. In other words, the more residents search for choice-related concepts, the more eco-friendly the state’s average score is.
Conclusion
What This Means

The more salient the idea of choice in people's minds, the more likely they are to support the environment — endorsing environmental initiatives, donating to environmental causes, and actively engaging with environmental content.

Policy Implication
A Smart, Low-Cost Nudge
Subtly enhancing the salience of choice in ads or communication messages is cost-effective and easily implementable — requiring no financial incentives, no regulatory change, and no major campaign overhaul. Just two words.
