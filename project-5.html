TL;DR
In online communities, people are more likely to consume a virtual gift when it is more expensive, sent by a high-status giver, or received by a high-status recipient. But status acts as a substitute for price — the more status is present, the less price matters.
Background
The Rise of Virtual Gifting

Virtual gifts — non-physical items purchased, sent, received, and consumed as gifts — are a fast-growing category in online platforms. From avatar accessories and apparel to home decor in virtual worlds, platforms like TikTok, Second Life, and Kim Kardashian Hollywood all offer virtual gift economies.

Despite their growing commercial importance, little is known about what drives recipients to actually consume these gifts. This study uses observational data from a European online virtual world to examine how gift price, giver status, and recipient status influence consumption decisions.

Methodology
Observational Data & Econometric Modeling
Data
19,654 gift consumption incidents with a consumption rate of 62%, drawn from a European online virtual world
Status
Measured by degree centrality — the number of friends a user has in the non-directional social network
Analysis
Binary probit model with the control function approach — to address endogeneity due to status acquisition
+ Heckman's two-step correction — to address endogeneity due to selection bias
Key Findings
Three Drivers of Consumption
+16%
Gift Price
Recipients are more likely to consume a virtual gift as the gift price increases by $0.25.
+13%
Giver Status
Recipients are more likely to consume when the giver's status increases by 0.1 unit of degree centrality.
+17%
Recipient Status
Recipients are more likely to consume when their own status increases by 0.1 unit of degree centrality.
Interaction Effects — Status as a Substitute for Price
↘
The marginal effect of gift price on consumption decreases as the giver's status or the recipient's status increases — high status reduces reliance on price as a signal of gift value.
↘
The marginal effect of giver status on consumption decreases as the recipient's status increases — high-status recipients are less swayed by the social signal of who sent the gift.
Note: Marginal effects are interpreted at the corresponding percentile of the moderating variable. For example, for a giver in the 95th percentile of status, a $0.25 price increase raises consumption probability by –0.05%, vs. +0.33% for a giver in the 5th percentile.
Managerial Implications
Two Strategic Directions
Implication 01
Smarter Gift Recommendation & Targeted Promotion
Integrate giver and recipient characteristics — particularly relative status between them — as features in gift recommendation systems and targeted promotion engines, supplementing existing price- and category-based signals.
For a high-status recipient receiving from a low-status giver, price becomes a stronger lever — promotions on premium gifts may be especially effective.
Implication 02
Nudge Status Acquisition & Increase Status Salience
Display user status through tiered status badges on profiles. Encourage status growth via membership, connection-building, and semantic priming that elevates perceived status.
Example message: "To our honored friend [name]: You have received a gift from the prestigious [giver]. We hope you enjoy it!"
