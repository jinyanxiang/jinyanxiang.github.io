TL;DR
Panic buying during COVID-19 was driven less by fear of scarcity and more by a desire to reduce shopping trips and minimize contact with others. It also served as a novel form of compensatory consumption — helping people cope with the anxiety and stress of the pandemic.
Background
What Is Panic Buying?

Panic buying refers to a phenomenon in which consumers purchase unusually large quantities of goods because they anticipate a crisis, crisis-caused price increases, or shortages.

During COVID-19, empty shelves — toilet paper, hand sanitizer, canned goods — became a defining image of the pandemic's early weeks.

But what actually drives this behavior?

Empty supermarket shelves, early COVID-19 pandemic, 2020

Key Findings
Cognitive & Emotional Drivers
🧠 Cognitive Factors
Safety, Not Scarcity, Is the Primary Driver
Although prior research emphasizes anticipated scarcity as the key motivator, increased purchase volume during COVID-19 was driven primarily by a safety concern — specifically, the desire to reduce shopping trips and minimize contact with strangers.
Additional cognitive factors that shaped the severity of panic buying:
🗺️
Local severity of the pandemic
🛒
Availability of grocery pickup or delivery services
🏠
Household size
⭐
Perceived criticality of the product category
📋
Individual capacity for planning ahead
❤️ Emotional Factors
Novel Finding
Panic Buying as Compensatory Consumption
Panic buying appears to serve as a novel form of compensatory consumption — a coping mechanism that helps individuals manage negative emotions and stressors arising from the pandemic.
When people feel a loss of control over their environment, stocking up provides a sense of agency, preparedness, and psychological security — even when the goods themselves may not be immediately necessary.
Policy Implication
What Policymakers & Retailers Can Do

Understanding that panic buying is driven by safety concerns and emotional coping — not just irrational fear of scarcity — changes how crises should be managed and communicated.

Recommended Action
Provide Emotional Support & Effective Coping Strategies
Governments, public health agencies, and retailers should offer adequate emotional support and disseminate effective coping strategies to help individuals navigate the uncertainties of future civil crises — reducing the anxiety that fuels compensatory stockpiling. Messaging that restores a sense of control and safety may be more effective than supply-focused reassurances alone.
